### SUBJECT: FLYING DISCS

Reference is made to letter dated September 11, 1947.

Above of Air Materiel Intelligence, United States Air Force, reported that four unidentified objects in flight over the city on 17 December 1947 was received from office in District 13, which is in the eastern portion of the city, at 5:21 p.m. The two fighters off Keesler Field at 5:23 p.m. radioed report District 13, which did further scout, to attempt to locate and identify the objects and that at 5:26 p.m. a report was received from the same occupied by Chief of Police, Keesler, reporting that they had spotted some objects, District 13, at 5:27 p.m. reported seeing nothing unusual in the area.

SA D. C. AGART, 4753 2nd Avenue Av., Portland, Oregon, advised that he was one of the two officers on duty in District 13 on September 17. He was in the vicinity of N. E. 25th Avenue and Broadway Coord when AGART and his follow officer noticed a number of saucer looking upward and following. Upon looking at the sky AGART observed the very bright reflection of the sun on some object which was momentarily made of silver metal. The object was coming from the east, made a wide turn over the city and disappeared to the south. AGART estimated the speed at about 150 miles per hour and the altitude at about 5000 feet. He watched it for a period of about two minutes and during that time it gradually diminished in such a manner so to appear that the angle was slowly changing, so that the sun was no longer reflected into AGART's eyes. He says that the altitude appeared to be about but that the plane was so bright that it could have hidden the true outline of the object. There was no motion visible — only steady flight. AGART believed it was an ordinary natural metallic reflection flying light of the afternoon sun.

SA G. H. LINDQUIST, District 13, Portland, Oregon was AGART's partner in District 13 on September 17. LINDQUIST states that he did not attempt to take the same course as that described by AGART. He pointed out that the speed, height, and size of the object were difficult to judge, because of the interpretation of the sun's reflection. However, he believed that the object which AGART described was about three miles away and about two miles high. He believes it was travelling at a very great speed from a somewhat southerly direction. At one time it appeared to him to be affiliated in shape, and did not seem to change form or size during its flight. It was an extremely brilliant light and had a pulsating appearance.
