LONDON, ONTARIO, TUESDAY, MARCH 29, 1966—36 PAGES

## Tracked by Radar
### UFOs Sighted Over Wide Section of District

An unidentified flying object was tracked briefly on radar by a U.S. Air Force base in Michigan last night, and more than a dozen other sightings were reported by Western Ontario residents.

A retired U.S. Marine Corps officer said yesterday "there is substantial evidence that they are observation devices controlled by a superior civilization."

Sightings of the objects were reported last night over an area stretching from Sarnia to Kintore, northwest of Woodstock. The mysterious objects came in a variety of forms, according to their observers. There was an orange one at Sarnia. And another giving off red, white and blue flashes. There was a red one in London and one with a red and white halo. The one at Kintore was a bright white light that intermittently faded to dull red, trailing it through the sky like mad and some of them were sitting stock-still in mid-air. Some were poking along and some were travelling slowly with intermittent bursts of speed.

A spokesman at the U.S. Air Force base in Belfridge, Mich., said one was tracked briefly on radar, but could not say what it was. Some observers said their sightings lasted for about seven minutes. Other reported keeping the object in sight for up to three hours.

The Free Press sent five men out Martian-hunting, two of them in a chartered plane, but found only two high-flying jet planes carrying the usual red and green running lights and leaving white vapor trails.

**Maj. Donald E. Keyhoe**, the Marine officer who advanced the outer-space-spy-in-the-sky theory on behalf of the National Committee on Aerial Phenomena called on the U.S. Government yesterday to release all the facts it has on UFO sightings.

"There is substantial evidence we are being observed by some sort of device which is far more advanced than anything we have and is controlled by a superior civilization," he told a press conference.

"Those things are real and under intelligent control," he added. He urged the air force to end public bungling and stop ridiculing the competent witnesses" who have seen them.

Keyhoe, who has written several books on the subject of UFO's, is director of the investigations committee.

The majority of the Western Ontario sightings came from London and Sarnia.

Mrs. J. Fielding, of 105 Oxford St. E., London, reported seeing a white light in the northeast moving toward Stratford at 7:25 p.m.

A Thorndale area man, Floyd Baxter, at 7:40 reported seeing an orange and yellow light moving very slowly in the northwest. He estimated it to be more than 1,000 feet high.

Fifteen minutes later at Kinore, six miles northeast of Thorndale, a mother and her two children saw a white light, "quite high in the sky, that seemed to be blinking."

Mrs. J. H. Haynes, of King Township, said it had some sound and we watched it for 10 minutes, anyway. It would brighten up, then it would dim down."

"It was moving ever so slowly," she added.

Her daughter, Wendy, 15-year-old Ingleside District Collegiate Institute student, said it was "a large, white light which kept dimming and getting brighter."

Larry, her 13-year-old brother, saw the object but he insisted that when it became dim "I could see a red color coming from it."

Mrs. George T. Dayus, of 20 Hutton Rd., came home from a cub meeting with three young boys at 8:20. He sighted the object moving southward in the sky.

"It appeared to be about 70 degrees above the horizon. We got a couple of neighbors to point at and discuss the object. Opinions on where it was varied wildly."

Op. Robert Symington, of the Sarnia police department, said "I saw it but I don't believe it. I didn't believe in anything until it's proved to me."

The object seemed to bob and weave almost like an orange colored balloon.

The sighting followed a similar experience Sunday night by Mrs. Harold Landry, of 550 Kingsway Drive, who watched an object glow red, blue, green and white for more than two hours.

### UFO SIGHTED BY LEWISTON PEOPLE
An unidentified flying object was spotted Saturday night by Lewiston-area residents.

Lewiston police said Patricia Landry, of 35 Tarr Ave. spotted the UFO at about 9:17 p.m. above her home.

Shortly after, police, the Landry woman and seven other persons spotted the object which was described as red, white and gray in color, oval in shape. It was said the UFO hovered for 40 minutes, then took off "look off" at a high rate of speed in an easterly direction.

## March 17, '66
### THE SUN-BULLETIN—Binghamton, N.Y.,
## Not star, says collector of flying saucer photos

The director of Unit 46 of the Amalgamated Flying Saucer Clubs of America is not convinced that the object sighted in the skies over Port Dickinson Tuesday night was the star Arcturus.

"I know enough about astronomy to know that a star does not behave in that manner," Gerald M. Reynolds of 608 State St. said last night.

Mr. Reynolds said he had talked with three persons who witnessed a strange object flashing multi-colored lights about 8:30 p.m. Tuesday. He hopes to talk with others who saw the object.

Mr. Reynolds, a construction foreman, is 60 years old. He has a large file of photographs and news clippings, relating to space flight.

He admits that all sightings are mysterious, and told of two persons who wanted him to tell his own strange sighting seen over Binghamton.

"One turned out to be the landing light of an Eastern plane ... and the other the light projection from a house on Ely Park hill," he laughed.

## (THE TELEGRAM, Toronto, Sat. March 26, 1966)
## Swamp gas still 'flying'

DETROIO — (UPI) — The U.S. Air Force closed its books today on the case of a "flying saucer" seen by midwest residents, blaming it all on swamp gas.

But some of the people who witnessed what they thought was a landing by a glowing, extra-terrestrial space craft, were not completely convinced the explanation was right.

And even while the two most spectacular Michigan sightings now wore the tag of an "official explanation," UFO sightings continued to pop up across the country, in rural areas and cities alike.

Dr. J. Allen Hynek, the top Air Force adviser on UFO sightings, concluded three days of on-the-scene investigation by telling a news conference that it appeared "very likely" swamp gas was responsible for most reports of strange hovering lights at Hillsdale in southern Michigan.

William Van Horn, Hillsdale civil defense director said he was "dissatisfied" with Hynek's explanation because the sighting was "too brief" and because Hynek sidestepped mention of the report from two college co-eds who said they saw the UFO close-up as it swept past their window.

Other "UFO" sightings meanwhile were reported from a drive-in restaurant in Des Moines; from some police officers in St. Louis; from a truck driver on a Michigan interstate highway; from a farmer in Toledo, Ohio; from a farmer in Upper Sandusky, Ohio; from a housewife at McComb, Miss.; a newsman at Bangor, Maine, who took a potshot at his object.

## Mont. Star, 4-19-66
## Photographed Flying Saucer, Picture Censored

**Associated Press**
RAVENNA, Ohio, April 19 — "We were closer, closer than I ever want to be again," said a deputy sheriff who chased an unidentified flying object from Ohio into Pennsylvania.

Hundreds of persons in both states reported seeing the brilliant object early Sunday morning.

Police Chief Gerald Buchert of Mantua, about eight miles north of Ravenna, said he took a picture of the object from his front yard but the Air Force told him not to release it.

Buchert said it looked like "two table saucers put together."

Portage County Deputy Sheriff Dale Spaur said he and his deputy, W. W. Neff, chased the object in separate cars and chased it 86 miles for an hour and a half, from near Ravenna to Conway, Pa., near Pittsburgh.

Spaur said he clocked it at speeds up to 103 miles per hour. From the ground Spaur said it looked like the head of a flashlight, about 40 feet wide and 15 feet high.

Spaur said the lines of the object were very distinct. "Somebody had control over it," he said. "It wasn't just floating around, It can maneuver."

## *THE DENVER POST* Friday, April 8, 1966 3
## Six Teens Tell of 'Chase' by Buzzing, Lighted UFOs
### POLICE CHIEF 'CONVINCED THEY SAW SOMETHING'

By ROBERT KISTLER
*Denver Post Staff Writer*

Six picnicking Denver teen-agers reported Thursday night they were chased for an hour by unidentified flying objects in Daniels Park south of Denver in Douglas County.

The high school students—three boys, three girls—told Police Chief John C. MacIvor of Littleton they witnessed unexplained, apparently "hovering" red, blue and white lights accompanied by a series of pulsating buzzing sounds which began about 9:30 p.m.

The teens, all 17, who described what they saw, hesitantly, were described by MacIvor as being "sincere, cold sober and a little shaken by what they saw."

The group appeared at MacIvor's office shortly before 11 p.m.

"I was real skeptical at first," MacIvor said. "But each of the kids called their parents—who knew they had been picnicking—to tell them where they were.

"I know what they saw," he said, "but I'm convinced they saw something."

Alan Scrivner, son of Mr. and Mrs. Russell Scrivner of 1040 Jackson St., a junior at East High School, gave the following account:

Scrivner and five friends drove to Daniels Park, 10-12 miles southwest of Littleton, about 8:30 p.m. They parked the car and walked about a city block down a small incline and up again to a stone shelter built in the side of a hill.

The group was sitting around a fire they'd built inside when they heard "what sounded like a man walking on top of the shelter." The roof can easily be reached on foot from the slanting hillside.

Scrivner and Don Otis, an East High junior, son of Mr. and Mrs. Elmo Otis, 1959 Fairfax St., stood on top of the shelter and shined a flashlight into the surrounding area. They saw nothing.

The two—leaving the others behind in the shelter—made their way through the underbrush back to the road. "I thought someone was messing around my car," Scrivner said.

"Just as we got to the car," he said, "both of us heard a real weird buzzing noise. It seemed to be all around us.

"As they reached the car, the youths saw "two red lights—about a foot each in diameter—about two feet off the ground and parallel to each other in the center of the reds."

The lights, about six feet apart, appeared to be in a field on the far side of the road "about 25 yards" from the car. The two, thinking it "might be two parked cars or something," crouched in the ditch and waited.

About three minutes later, they again approached the shelter. They saw and heard nothing. They returned to the shelter.

The four in the shelter said that, while the two had been gone, they'd heard the same "footsteps-like" sound on the roof again. Two of the girls said they'd also seen "a tall man, who might've been wearing a raincoat," dart through the light showing through the window into the area. They also said they heard his footsteps.

The group decided to leave.

As they were walking back, the Otis turned and saw "a real bright flash behind the hill over the shelter and it lit up the whole area."

### Buzzing 'All Around'

The ominous buzzing began again. No direction, "just all around—never heard anything like it in my life," Scrivner said. Shortly after as the group neared the car, they all agreed to look at the same time.

Four lights hovered in the sky, about three-quarters of a mile away. The lights were stationary and went from bright to dim. A third—white—light appeared to rise up and down between the blues and "lit up the area."

No outline—other than the lights—was visible.

They perched atop the car's hood and watched the blue and white lights "on and off" for about an hour half. The lights appeared in back of the shelter—in the opposite direction from the first lights seen in the field.

Twenty minutes after the blues appeared, another set of red lights appeared "off to the left of the others and appeared about a block away." These hovered near the ground. The buzzing continued.

The night was overcast with no stars visible but "real clear beneath the clouds where the lights were." As the third set of lights hovered, the youths agreed they could see a dark outline between the lights.

### 'Car Didn't Work Right'

The youngsters piled in the car—a 1964 Ford—and started out of the park.

"I'm sure they'll think we're nuts," Scrivner said later, "but my car didn't work right. I could get it up to about 30 (miles an hour) then it would cut out like someone turned the ignition off. Then it would start up again."

Scrivner also complained that the radio was "playing nothing but static" while they left the immediate area of the shelter.

As they drove off, two of the girls and Otis saw what appeared to be "a large, oval circle of light—it covered the whole road—following the car."

Mary Zolar, daughter of Mr. and Mrs. Jack A. Zolar, 945 Fairfax St., a senior at East, was in the back seat with two girls.

"It was huge and glowing," she said, "and came right up to the back of the car, then went out. It lasted about three seconds."

As the car left the park area, it quit "missing out" and the radio played normally, Scrivner said.

### Called Sheriff's Office

The youths stopped at a service station and called the Arapahoe County sheriff's office, then went to MacIvor's office.

MacIvor said "one of the impressive things about their story" was all six didn't claim to have seen the exact same thing at the same time.

"And a couple of them are real shook up," he added.

The operations officer at Lowry Air Force Base said early Friday he'd received a call apparently from the Arapahoe sheriff's office concerning the sighting.

Others in the group were Michael Simington, son of Mr. and Mrs. James E. Simington, 773 Jacks nSt., a junior at East; Patty Retherford, daughter of Mr. and Mrs. Neal Retherford, 742 Harrison St., a junior at Mapleton High School, and Kaye Hurley, daughter of Mr. and Mrs. Lloyd Hurley, 5736 Locust St., a senior at Machette High.

Two other UFO sightings were reported in the state, one in Delta and the other in Denver.

Delta Police Chief D. C. Morgan, patrolman Ernest Markley and reporter Don Prather of the Delta Independent watched an object which was "glowing, green in color and very bright." The object was observed for almost two hours before it disappeared from sight straight up.

In Denver, Terry Datz, 19, a University of Denver student, said he and Dennis Sullivan of Mt. Vernon, N.Y., spotted about seven UFOs which appeared "garbage can lid size."

The students said the objects disappeared into the clouds after about 10 minutes.
