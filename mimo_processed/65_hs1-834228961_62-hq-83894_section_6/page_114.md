## A NUCLEAR PHYSICIST EXPOSES FLYING SAUCERS

**"There is no longer any need for secrecy,"** says Navy scientist, after finding that his own research started the "saucers"

By RICHARD WILSON *Chief of LOOK Washington Bureau*

### Page 60

*Image: A magazine/newspaper article page titled “A NUCLEAR PHYSICIST EXPOSES FLYING SAUCERS” with two embedded black-and-white photographs: one of a balloon-like spherical object against a dark background and one of a large skyhook balloon being launched on the ground; the right page also contains a small photo of a balloon over an aircraft carrier with a caption.*

**Pictured above is a Skyhook balloon 77,000 feet over Minneapolis. This first published photo of a Skyhook at that altitude was taken through a refracting telescope. To Dr. Urner Liddel, chief of the nuclear-physics branch of the Office of Naval Research, it is the first tangible bit of his explanation of the "flying saucers" seen soaring over the skies of this country. Dr. Liddel's field of nuclear research is concerned with the study of cosmic rays in the outer atmosphere—and Skyhook is one of the instruments aloft. At the right, being launched, the balloon is partially inflated. Nearing the limit of its ascent, above, it clearly resembles a "flying saucer."**

> The literal-minded FBI, skeptical but determined, could not let the flying-saucer excitement go by without getting to the bottom of it. And the U. S. Navy, responsible for defending the American skies could not be ignored.
>
> A 10-page report by the nuclear physics branch of the Office of Naval Research has given the answer.
>
> Flying saucers were, and are, undeniably real. They are part of a basic research program of the Federal Government which is as important, if not so dramatic, as the visitation from Mars described by an imaginative publicist.
>
> A flying saucer is just a huge balloon, 100 feet in diameter, called a "Skyhook." It is seen by earthlings traveling at speeds up to 200 miles an hour.
>
> These balloons are carrying delicate instruments to plumb the secrets of the cosmos in the dizzy reaches of a 100,000-foot height where the atmosphere reaches the vanishing point.
>
> The instruments on the balloons observe and measure the countless explosions of atoms in the far reaches of the sky, and cosmic particles hurtling by the billions from the cold reaches of outer space.
>
> The balloons also seek the secret of how matter is put together by recording how it is blown apart. And, finally, of how the countless atomic explosions taking place silently, constantly, and for the most part unobserved at the fringe of the earth's atmosphere may be reproduced under controlled conditions.
>
> This is atomic research aimed not at producing bombs, but at developing practical power from the decomposition of the atom.
>
> Dr. Urner Liddel, chief of the nuclear physics branch of the Office of Naval Research, is in charge of the Skyhook-Flying Saucer project.

### No "Saucers" Without ONR

> "When this project first began," he said, "it was thought that there was no longer any need for secrecy on a scientific basis. And certainly, there is no longer any need to keep the public in the dark about what flying saucers really are."
>
> "If we are completely successful in our research with these balloons, which so many people have called flying saucers, we will find out how to tap the atom for its energy. It is not too much to hope that some day we will learn enough to cause the atomic reaction under conditions as we want them—not as they exist in the hydrogen bomb."
>
> "Some day we may learn enough so that we can pour a cup of water into a reactor the size of an average room and draw off from it in cables enough energy to heat a large city."
>
> The Office of Naval Research, where Dr. Liddel is sponsoring the cosmic experiments, is doing its part, in peace as well as in war, with a $10,000,000-a-year program of basic and applied research. The Liddel report is considered the first thorough, official explanation of the flying-saucer phenomenon.
>
> As far as Dr. Liddel is concerned personally, he considers his answer incontrovertibly right.
>
> One of the main objections to supporting Dr. Liddel's findings is that flying saucers or flying disks were unheard of until early this year when the Office of ONR's experiments in the stratosphere became public. There were only two reports last year that strange objects were seen in the skies. These reports multiplied in 1948, when Skyhooks were put in the air in large numbers, and reached a climax in 1949. They diminished in 1950 when the number of Skyhook balloon flights were reduced.

### Ionization Chambers in the Sky

> Observers have agreed that the best time of day to see what is called a flying saucer is at dawn or dusk.
>
> You may be standing on a plain in New Mexico or Montana. You may be flying a plane at 10,000 feet over Alabama. And there in the distance above you but far from you, is a strange light—still, it may be moving or just hovering.
>
> But there it is. You see it. Your companions see it. The thing is round. It appears cup-shaped. It may appear to have a strong glow on one side or to be trailing an exhaust.
>
> What you have seen may be Flight 10, Balloon No. 3 of the Brookhaven National Laboratory at 84,300 feet carrying a 167-pound payload consisting of a cloud chamber, a beta-ray spectrometer, an ionization chamber and photographic equipment.
>
