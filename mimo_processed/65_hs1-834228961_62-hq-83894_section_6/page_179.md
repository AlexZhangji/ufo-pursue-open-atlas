# TWIN DAY FOR BEDSERS

EVEN the gasometers winked broadly and the bars buzzed with hilarious bowler-hatted gentlemen of South London as mighty Yorkshire crashed with an unholy crash at the Oval.

Latecomers could not believe the cocky, so-often ominous, Surrey colours, for a meagre 13, and among the early slain England's captain, Len Hutton, who scored 9.

A crowd of 25,000 people crammed into the ground—were they happy in the sunshine? They engendered a Surrey magnificent bowling achievement.

The toast over the tinkling glasses was the Bedser twins, who had celebrated their 34th birthday the previous day.

### Test-match style

Brother Alec, in his fiercest, most reminiscent form, and in Test-match style, took five Yorkshire wickets for just over ten runs apiece. Eric, who has come into his own as an opening batsman, scored 67 and did his share in the Surrey attack to-morrow.

Yorkshire have a job on, for they are 134 for three at the loss of six wickets and it took T. J. Bush more than four rounds to gather together his 62 runs.

Yorkshire's left-handers, slow bowlers, Arthur Wood, saved the side from dire disgrace though Wilson, who scored 50, was lucky not to be out when seven.

### GILROY'S SPORT GOSSIP

MORE than the world's best athletes will be tested in the 1952 Olympiad, due to start at Helsinki next week. The Olympic Games themselves will be on trial.

We British, who have so loyally adhered through two wars to the ideals which inspired the Games will know if we have been sentimental fools. We have been.

This Olympics will probably resemble a humble duty performed between America and Russia. No nation has a greater duty to go through with it than those two great rivals—think of each other in the world of politics.

This international political rival will be played out in a sporting arena and justify the cynics who allege nationalism is rampant in the Olympic Games?

### Nazi vulgarity

OLYMPIC prestige slumped when the 1936 Games held by Berlin were cynically used by Germany to boost their particular political creed at home.

Of course the staging and organisation was spectacular and the athletes were marvellous. But it left a sour taste of vulgarity and turned British stomachs.

The 1948 Games at Wembley restored the Olympic ideal to its pedestal. Remember, we were rather hard up then than we are now.

We struggled through somehow. Our arrangements did not have the panoply or luxury as the Berlin or Los Angeles Olympics. But the real spirit of the Games. And the world went away happier than before.

A LITTLE Finland, with only 4,000,000 people, have put up a magnificent show in the staging of the Games. The Finns will be determined there will be no repetition of Berlin.

### Dead-bat technique

It did not raise much above that for nearly all day. At times it dropped as low as five in half an hour.

Beyond the fact that Bush once had to duck to avoid the falling ball the Oxford attack was very fast, but not more than medium.

The Cambridge fielding was better than we have seen it for some time and it is impossible to avoid the feeling that the big crowd—and the weather—deserved something better than so much negative defence and dead-bat technique.

Towards the finish P. D. S. Blake and W. M. Mitchell swung the game round by a more profitable process, even if it happened later than majority opinion reckoned it should have done.

## Oxford slow march

### By ROLAND ALLEN

IT was the spin of Robin Marlar, bowling his accurate off breaks up the hill in the second innings yesterday which worried Oxford's better batsmen much more than the medium pace of D. G. Cowdey, the young Cambridge wicket-keeper, who has a wide range of strokes, only occasionally showed them to the large crowd. He made 55, which took him 175 minutes.

He was in for 32 minutes before he scored, mostly defending.

He struck seven fours, two of them over the deep long-on boundary from the straight drive.

We also recall such a stroke made by an Oxford skipper. Bush, who made many more, seemed that although their fair share of the scoring runs the Oxford run-getting rate through it all was below 30 an hour.

### World's best equalled

Records fell in the relay events. Australia's young Lovelock ran the quarter-mile in 46.8sec., equalling the world's best set up by a South African team.

Marjorie Jackson, who ran the 100 and 200 metres earlier, won the 100 metres (11.7sec.) and the 200 metres (23.6sec.) fastest distances by a woman in Britain.

In the 4 x 440 yards relay the Jamaica team broke the British Empire record of 3mins. 13.6sec., by 2secs.

The England-Wales team won the triangular match with 110 points. Scotland scored 66, Ireland 34.
