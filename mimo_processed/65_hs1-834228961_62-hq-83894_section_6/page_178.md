# LITTLE MO BECOMES MIGHTY MO

Sharp's THE WORD FOR TOFFEE

'COPYBOY' MAKES THE BIG TENNIS NEWS OF YEAR

By HAROLD LAWRENCE

MAUREEN CONNOLLY, the 17-year-old "copyboy" (she fetches reporters tea and sandwiches) of a California newspaper, is the biggest thing in tennis to-day. In her first year as a senior she joined the great few who have won the women's singles at Wimbledon, at her first attempt.

To-day she will be perfectly timed to meet Louise Brough in the women's singles peak form when most others are going down. In Australia this year, in April and May, Little Mo defeated three successive Australian champions to win the championship.

### Coming back

We may as well get used to the idea that we are going to see a youngster stepping into the shoes of the great Helen Wills. The tennis comparisons are painfully obvious – the right arm, the compact, powerful frame, the determination.

Two months ago she won the hard-fought London championship final with Mrs. Doris Hart, after being several games behind. She had to scratch from Wimbledon when she cut her knee playing on a rough court to scratch from Wimbledon when she cut her knee playing on a rough court. Most of her experts thought Maureen had had it. She won her opening match to-day and a few days later was in doubt for the women's singles, the two-year ambition of her life.

With conventional grass-court service, it was not long before she was beating the top-seeded players. But a few months ago, Louise still looked the great favourite. Then Louise became ill and Maureen became a certainty for the title. But the champion's fighting spirit was not to be underestimated.

Louise led two love in the second set, but was pulled up for foot-faulting – an incident which might have rattled little Miss Confidence. Maureen forced a tie, which throws out that last burst of effort in the opening of the second set, mostly with conventional grass-court tactics and three service aces.

# 50 YARDS AWAY I SAW TWO MEN TAKE OFF IN A 'FLYING SAUCER'

From ANTONY TERRY, Berlin, Saturday

ARMED with a sensational affidavit sworn by an eye-witness, intelligence officers in West Berlin are today investigating one of the most amazing "flying saucer" stories ever reported.

Evidence that a "weird contraption" – "like a 30ft. warming pan without a handle, and with a 10ft. conning tower" – took off with a crew of two from a forest clearing in the Soviet zone is being studied at the highest levels in the hope to answer the big question: Are "flying saucers" a secret new Russian invention?

It is extremely difficult to get confirmation of this remarkable story because most West German editions of the *Sunday Graphic* had been confiscated.

But I can now reveal that the witness, a dark-haired, 48-year-old ex-Mayor, Otto Lehnke, lives in a village near Meiningen. He had escaped to the West only a year ago, leaving his wife and six children.

### Men in metal

I can now reveal that in the company of West Berlin intelligence officers I interviewed the 48-year-old ex-Mayor. Otto Lehnke. He swore a solemn and formal affidavit before a lawyer.

"I was riding home on my bicycle," he said, "when a tyred track made a strange, hissing sound, with something about 150 yards away in a clearing. I saw a dim, half light. I took it for a machine toward Hackel, near the village of Gattendorf."

Then I realised that my first impression had been incorrect. The thing I had mistaken for a machine was something about 150 yards away in a clearing. I saw a dim, half light. I took it for a deer cautiously, but I came to within 150 yards of the clearing."

### Footnote from Vickers Chief Designer

Mr. George Edwards, Chief Designer for Vickers-Armstrongs, had this to say about the West German story:

"It is the idea of a circular plan. Before long, I am sure we shall have this type of thing. We called it the Flying Doughnut. It was a great idea, but somehow we never got beyond the money and the need."

"What struck me was that from Herr Lehnke's description it may be that the 'conning tower' would appear to be retractable and would have to retract into the body in order to provide some form of protection."

Many variants are possible, too—they could be a form of propulsion. I should have to reserve judgement about the story, although, to believe in the story, I would have to be shown one myself!"
